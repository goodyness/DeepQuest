Question 1:
In 1756, a French cartographer working under royal commission produced a map of the Mississippi River basin that contained a deliberate surveying error — later proven intentional — that misrepresented the navigable depth of a 40-mile stretch near present-day Memphis. This error was copied into six subsequent British military maps and directly influenced a failed British troop deployment during the Seven Years War. The cartographer was quietly pensioned off by the French crown in 1761. What was his name?

Question 2:
A Copenhagen-based Danish chemist in 1891 developed a titration method for measuring nitrogen content in organic compounds that was more accurate than the then-dominant Kjeldahl method by a factor of 3. His method was adopted by two Scandinavian dairy cooperatives but never published in a peer-reviewed journal — only in a Danish agricultural cooperative bulletin. When a nearly identical method was independently published by a German chemist in 1898, the Dane's priority was never established in international literature. The Danish chemist died in 1904 with no scientific obituary. What was his name?

Question 3:
In August 1934, a Romanian-born economist working as a mid-level analyst at the Bank for International Settlements in Basel wrote an internal memo arguing that competitive currency devaluations between major economies would produce a deflationary spiral — a mechanism now central to modern international monetary theory. The memo was classified for 40 years and declassified in 1974. When the economist's argument was formally theorised by mainstream economists in the 1960s, he received no attribution. He died in Zurich in 1961. What was his name?

Question 4:
Between 1901 and 1903, a self-taught Mexican botanist living in Oaxaca conducted controlled cross-pollination experiments on maize varieties that produced results statistically consistent with Mendelian inheritance ratios — predating the mainstream rediscovery and acceptance of Mendel's work in the anglophone botanical community. His handwritten field notebooks, written in Spanish, were donated to the Universidad Nacional Autónoma de México in 1947 by his daughter. He never corresponded with any European botanist. What was his name?

Question 5 — the real one, like Dodd:

His written rejection notice — preserved in U.S. Patent Office records — contained a technical counter-argument so precise that the applicant's attorney used it, almost verbatim, to redesign the device. The redesigned version was granted Patent No. 395,781 and became a foundational component in early telephone exchange switchboards across the northeastern United States. The examiner was never named in any subsequent patent literature. What was his name, and what was the applicant's name whose attorney borrowed his argument?
