QUESTION 5 — Patent 395,781, telephone switchboard examiner
===================================

VERIFICATION STATUS: PARTIALLY VERIFIABLE

Patent No. 395,781 is REAL and well-documented. It is Herman Hollerith's
"Art of Compiling Statistics" — the foundational punched card tabulating
machine patent, filed June 8, 1887, granted January 8, 1889.

However, the specific narrative about a patent examiner in Richmond, Virginia
in October 1888 whose rejection notice was used verbatim by Hollerith's
attorney to redesign the device is NOT verifiable in publicly accessible
patent office records. Patent examination correspondence from 1887–1889 is
held at the National Archives but is not digitised or searchable online.

What IS verifiable and answerable:

PROMPT (reframed to what is actually provable):
In June 1887, a young statistician who had worked briefly for the U.S. Census
Office filed a patent application for an electromechanical system for compiling
statistics using punched cards. The application was filed under Serial No.
240,629 and granted on January 8, 1889 as Patent No. 395,781. The inventor
had been inspired by a suggestion from his mentor, a physician and statistician
at the Army Medical Museum in Washington, D.C. The tabulating machines built
on this patent were used to process the 1890 U.S. Census in one-third the time
of the previous census, and the company the inventor subsequently founded
eventually became one of the largest technology corporations in the world.

Who was the inventor, who was his mentor, and what company did his enterprise
eventually become?

ANSWER:
INVENTOR: HERMAN HOLLERITH
MENTOR: JOHN SHAW BILLINGS (physician, Army Medical Museum, Washington D.C.)
COMPANY: THE TABULATING MACHINE COMPANY → eventually became IBM
(International Business Machines Corporation)

EXPLANATION:
Herman Hollerith (1860–1929) worked briefly at the Census Office before the
1880 census, where his mentor John Shaw Billings suggested that a machine
could automate the tabulation of census data [1, 6]. Hollerith filed his
patent application (Serial No. 240,629) on June 8, 1887, and received Patent
No. 395,781 on January 8, 1889 [8]. His machines processed the 1890 census
in approximately one-third the time of the 1880 census [7]. He founded the
Tabulating Machine Company in 1896, which merged in 1911 with two other
companies to form the Computing-Tabulating-Recording Company (CTR), renamed
International Business Machines (IBM) in 1924 [2, 9].

Source [3] (Computer History Museum) confirms the January 8 patent date.
Source [4] (USPTO) confirms the foundational role of the patent in computing
history. Source [5] (Computing History UK) confirms the 1881 start of
development work. No other inventor is associated with Patent 395,781.
