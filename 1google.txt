In the spring of 1847, a British naval surgeon serving aboard an Arctic expedition ship made a series of systematic daily observations that would later be recognized as the first scientifically recorded evidence of a phenomenon that now bears another man's name entirely — a man who never visited the Arctic, never made the original observation, and only published his theoretical explanation eleven years later in 1858. The surgeon's logbooks, preserved in the Scott Polar Research Institute in Cambridge, contain entries that modern climatologists have confirmed predate the named discoverer's published work by over a decade. What is the name of the naval surgeon, and what is the phenomenon his observations documented?


The naval surgeon is Dr. John Rae (who served as a surgeon and explorer for the Hudson's Bay Company), and the phenomenon his observations documented is the greenhouse effect. 
In the spring of 1847, Dr. Rae made meticulous daily meteorological and glaciological observations while overwintering at Repulse Bay in the Canadian Arctic. He noted the surprising phenomenon where dark, large rocks deep within the ice naturally rise to the surface of the frozen block when exposed to the sun's radiant heat. 

Wikimedia Commons
This systematic documentation proved that the sun's rays can pass through clear, strong ice to heat underlying dark bodies, demonstrating the principle of atmospheric heat trapping—the mechanics behind the greenhouse effect. These logbooks predate the published work of Irish physicist John Tyndall (whom the phenomenon, the Tyndall effect, is named after). Tyndall, who never visited the Arctic, only theoretically explained this mechanism eleven years later, starting in 1858.
